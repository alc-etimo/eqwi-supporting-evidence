Occupation Mapping: a transferable skill

To optimize my own LinkedIn presence, I systematically researched job descriptions to understand which terms recruiters actually used. For example, "Localization Specialist" vs. "Localization Expert" vs. "Localization Manager" — and applied Boolean operators (OR) in my searches to normalize equivalent concepts and surface every aligned opportunity. This is the same reasoning used for occupation mapping: recognizing that different surface strings can refer to the same underlying role, then collapsing them onto a canonical entry in a controlled taxonomy. It is a transferable skill — the domain changes, the logic doesn't.

Localization Specialist OR Localization Expert OR Localization Manager
↓ Normalized to a canonical occupation → broader, cleaner search

IBGE — Brazilian Census · Data Validation

As a field-data supervisor for the IBGE (Instituto Brasileiro de Geografia e Estatística, the Brazilian federal statistics agency running the national Census), I reviewed thousands of household forms collected by agents. My core task was exactly this: review, validate and reclassify information correctly — catching swapped fields (a profession written in the "Age" column, family income entered in the "Number of residents" column), reconciling inconsistencies, and returning clean, standardized records to the central database.
