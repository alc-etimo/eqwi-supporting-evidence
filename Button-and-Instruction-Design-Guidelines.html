1. Instruction Writing Guidelines ▾
35%
Reduction in cognitive load by focusing on one task at a time (Action Antecedent Principle)
20%
Lower cognitive effort using sequential logic (Chunking & Progressive Disclosure)
40%
More effective using active voice and imperative mood — reduces ambiguity (Plain Language)
  • Principles:
    • Place the instruction on the same line as the answer in the spreadsheet.
    • Use imperative mood for direct instructions (e.g., "Confirm" instead of "Confirmar").
  • Rationale:
    • Instruction → Orientation: Separating instructions from questions reduces cognitive overload. The brain processes "what to do" and "how to respond" sequentially.
    • Sequential Logic: Aligns guided content with operator mental models, reducing TMA (Average Handling Time).
    • Active Voice & Consistency: Imperative mood is up to 40% more effective for action instructions (Plain Language Association International, 2023).
  • How to apply:
    • Instructions always before the question.
    • Keep instructions short and direct.
    • Convert infinitive verbs to imperative ("Confirmar" → "Confirm").

2. Question Writing Guidelines ▾
-30%
Cognitive processing time with specific, direct questions (Nielsen Norman Group, 2020)
-45%
Rework rate by eliminating ambiguity in critical questions (Google UX Research, 2023)
+52%
Response accuracy when question and buttons share the same semantic logic (IBM Design Language, 2022)
  • Principles:
    • Questions must be direct, specific, and contextual.
    • Avoid repeating information already known in the title or context.
    • Use the same semantic logic between question and response options.
  • Rationale:
    • Ambiguity Reduction: Vague questions require +200ms of cognitive processing (NN/g).
    • Semantic Alignment: When question and buttons share logic, accuracy increases 52%.
    • Linguistic Economy: Removing unnecessary words accelerates comprehension by 30% (Plain Language).
  • How to apply:
    • Identify the essential information the operator needs to collect.
    • Formulate the question focusing on that information, without repeating context.
    • Ensure response buttons are the logical continuation of the question.
    • Test with operators: if they need to re-read, the question needs simplifying.

⚡ The Central Problem: Semantic Discrepancy

There is semantic discrepancy when the question and buttons don't use the same language logic. In critical flows, this creates doubt: "What exactly am I answering by clicking here?"

— UX & Operational Foundation
📊 Internal Research Data (Apr/2025)

"Even with repetition, operators prefer more context in answers."

This means click safety is prioritized over visual conciseness.

🔑 Critical Operational Principle

"In critical flows, the operator cannot rely on reading the question. They need to glance at the button and be absolutely certain of the action they are recording. The button must be the single source of truth."

— UX & Operational Rationale

✅ BEST PRACTICES
  • User language: Use terms the operator uses daily (consult speech analytics reports)
  • Visual consistency: Keep standardized colors and sizes
  • Logical grouping: Organize by related categories
  • Concise text: Be clear without being too long (max 3 words)
  • Strategic ordering: Place the most frequent options first
❌ AVOID
  • Technical jargon: Prefer "SITE" over "E-COMMERCE"
  • Unfamiliar acronyms: Use "PHONE SALES" instead of "TV"
  • Ambiguity: Avoid "OTHER" without context
  • Too many options: Max 5 buttons per screen
  • Long texts: Break into multiple lines if necessary
  • Not respecting taxonomy: Always check the THESA repository
